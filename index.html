What does Buffalo really owe?

Most of what Buffalo owes isn't debt, and that's the encouraging part. Here's the short, plain version, straight from the city's own books.

−$1.56B
owed beyond what the city has in the bank, and most of it is unfunded retiree benefits, not debt

The city's official bottom line is already negative, about −$590 million. Count only money Buffalo can actually spend, and the gap grows to −$1.56 billion. The biggest piece by far is retiree health care the city promised but never set money aside for (about $1.2 billion). Its actual debt is a much smaller part.

And one cost never reaches the books at all: fixing worn-out roads, pipes, and buildings. About half their value is used up; putting them right would run an estimated $1.7–$2.2 billion.

Deliberately rough numbers, about −$30,000 per household in total.

Good news first: borrowing isn't really the problem. Buffalo's debt is small and well-managed. The big number is mostly one promise the city hasn't funded yet: retiree health care. So the real questions are simpler:

1. Is it funding its promises?
Not yet. Retiree health care (~$1.2B) is the biggest thing it owes, and it's mostly unfunded.
2. Is it caring for what it owns?
Unclear, because the city has never added up what good repair would cost.
3. How much should the city borrow?
A genuinely hard call, and one being argued in court right now. It depends on what the money builds and whether the city keeps it up, which is why the repair number matters.
The easy first step

Ask the city for one number

What would it cost to bring everything Buffalo owns back to good repair? The city tracks the wear but never adds up the bill. That one number tells everyone, residents and officials alike, whether the city is investing too little, too much, or in the right places. It takes one Council member to ask.
